# S1: Derivation of the Physical Proposition Criterion

**Author:** James D. Longmire
**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Status:** Technical Supplement to LRT-MASTER
**Addresses:** The gap between L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction and operational distinguishability

---

## Abstract

The Physical Proposition Criterion (PPC) is the governing bridge principle of Logic Realism Theory. It states that a claim counts as a physical proposition if and only if its truth-value states are operationally distinguishable. This supplement derives the PPC from L<sub>3</sub>'s constitutive status, making explicit the inference that the master document presents but does not fully develop. The derivation proceeds through four stages: (1) the nature of L<sub>3</sub> as constitutive rather than regulative, (2) the content requirements each law imposes, (3) why those requirements entail operational distinguishability, and (4) the scope and limits of the result.

---

## 1. The Problem

### 1.1 What the Master Document Claims

Section 1.3 of LRT-MASTER states:

> "For P to satisfy Identity, it must have determinate content: there must be something that P asserts. For P to satisfy Non-Contradiction, P-true and P-false must be distinct states of affairs. For P to satisfy Excluded Middle, there must be a determinate truth value. All three requirements entail operational distinguishability: without a possible measurement distinguishing P-true from P-false, there is no determinate content, no distinction, no truth value."

The epistemic status is marked ARGUED. The concern: the inference from "L<sub>3</sub> requirements" to "operational distinguishability" is asserted but not derived. A critic could accept that P must have determinate content, distinct truth-value states, and a determinate truth value, while denying that these require operational access. This supplement closes that gap.

### 1.2 The Critic's Position

The coherent objection: physical reality may outstrip all possible operational access. There could be facts about configurations that satisfy L₃ (determinate, non-contradictory, bivalent) without any measurement capable of distinguishing them. Call this position **Operational Transcendence (OT)**.

OT is not obviously incoherent. A hidden variable theorist might hold that particle positions are determinate facts even when no measurement could reveal them. A structural realist might hold that relational facts between distant systems are determinate even when no local measurement could access them.

LRT's task is not to refute OT by showing it contains a logical contradiction. The task is to show that within LRT's framework, OT is unrealizable: accepting X ≡ [L<sub>3</sub> : I<sub>∞</sub> : A] commits one to denying OT.

## 2. The Constitutive Status of L<sub>3</sub>

### 2.1 Regulative vs. Constitutive

A **regulative** principle governs how we reason about a domain that exists independently of the principle. Classical logic is often treated as regulative: the world exists; logic tells us how to reason correctly about it.

A **constitutive** principle defines what counts as an element of the domain. A configuration in A<sub>Ω</sub> is not a pre-existing thing that L<sub>3</sub> then filters; L<sub>3</sub> is what makes it a configuration at all.

LRT's central claim is that L<sub>3</sub> is constitutive. This is not a novel metaphysical proposal but a clarification of what logical realism has always entailed: the Laws of Logic are not conventions of thought but structures of reality.

### 2.2 The Constitutive Claim Unpacked

To say L<sub>3</sub> is constitutive is to say:

1. **Identity** is not a relation we discover between a thing and itself. It is what makes something a thing. Without self-identity, there is no bearer of properties, no subject of predication, nothing to reason about.

2. **Non-Contradiction** is not a constraint we impose on our descriptions. It is what makes description possible. A subject that both has and lacks property P in the same respect at the same time has no determinate character. It is not a strange thing; it is no thing.

3. **Excluded Middle** is not a logical preference for bivalence. It is what makes propositions about physical configurations differ from expressions of uncertainty. If P is a proposition about a configuration c, then c either has the property P ascribes or it doesn't. There is no third option at the level of what c is.

### 2.3 The Consequence for Physical Propositions

A proposition P about a configuration c is a claim that c has (or lacks) some property. For P to be a physical proposition, c must be in A<sub>Ω</sub> (the actualized domain). For c to be in A<sub>Ω</sub>, c must satisfy L<sub>3</sub>.

The question is what L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires of the property P ascribes. The next section derives this requirement for each law.

## 3. Content Requirements of L<sub>3</sub>

### 3.1 Identity: Determinate Content

**The Law:** c = c.

**What It Requires:** For Identity to hold, c must be self-identical. This means c has a determinate character: there is something that c is. If c had no determinate character, there would be nothing for c to be identical to.

**Implication for P:** If P asserts "c has property F," then F must pick out a determinate aspect of c's character. A property that picks out nothing determinate is not a property of c; P fails to make a claim about c.

**The Question:** What makes F determinate?

**Answer:** F is determinate if and only if c's having-F differs from c's lacking-F. If having-F and lacking-F were indistinguishable, F would not pick out any aspect of c's character. It would be an empty predicate, not a property.

**Interim Conclusion:** Identity requires that the property P ascribes makes a difference to c's character. Difference-making is the content of determinacy.

### 3.2 Non-Contradiction: Distinct Truth-Value States

**The Law:** ¬(P ∧ ¬P).

**What It Requires:** P-true and P-false cannot both obtain. This means P-true and P-false are distinct states of affairs. If they were the same state of affairs, the conjunction P ∧ ¬P would be trivially true (or trivially meaningless).

**Implication for P:** The truth-value of P corresponds to a real difference in c's state. P-true is one state; P-false is another; they are not the same.

**The Question:** What makes P-true and P-false distinct states?

**Answer:** States are distinct if and only if something differs between them. This is just Identity applied to states: state s₁ ≠ state s₂ iff there is some respect in which s₁ and s₂ differ.

**Interim Conclusion:** Non-Contradiction requires that P's truth-value states differ in some respect. This respect is what P is about. If there is no differing respect, P has no content.

### 3.3 Excluded Middle: Determinate Truth Value

**The Law:** P ∨ ¬P.

**What It Requires:** Either P is true or P is false; there is no third option. This means P has a determinate truth value. The configuration c either has property F or it doesn't.

**Implication for P:** There must be a fact of the matter about P's truth value. If there were no fact of the matter, Excluded Middle would not apply to P, and P would not be a proposition about a configuration in A_Ω.

**The Question:** What makes there be a fact of the matter?

**Answer:** A fact of the matter exists when the difference between P-true and P-false is grounded in c's character. If c's character does not ground the difference, there is no fact for P to be true or false about.

**Interim Conclusion:** Excluded Middle requires that c's character determines P's truth value. This is the same requirement as Identity and Non-Contradiction approached from a different angle: c must have a determinate character that grounds the truth or falsity of propositions about it.

## 4. From Difference-Making to Operational Distinguishability

### 4.1 The Consolidated Requirement

From Section 3, L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires:

- P's property F makes a difference to c's character (Identity)
- P-true and P-false are distinct states of affairs (Non-Contradiction)
- c's character determines which state obtains (Excluded Middle)

All three converge on: **there is a difference between c-with-F and c-without-F**.

### 4.2 The Operational Question

The question now is: what does "difference" mean?

Here is where LRT's framework becomes load-bearing. I<sub>∞</sub> is defined as the space of all representable configurations, structured by distinguishability. The distinguishability metric D is:

$$D(s_1, s_2) = \sup_M |P_M(s_1) - P_M(s_2)|_{TV}$$

where M ranges over all physically admissible measurements.

Two configurations are distinct (D > 0) if and only if there exists a measurement M that yields different probability distributions for them. Two configurations are identical (D = 0) if no measurement distinguishes them.

### 4.3 The Inference

**Premise 1:** L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires that P-true and P-false are distinct states of affairs (from Section 3).

**Premise 2:** States of affairs in A<sub>Ω</sub> are configurations in I<sub>∞</sub> (A<sub>Ω</sub> ⊆ I<sub>∞</sub>).

**Premise 3:** Configurations in I<sub>∞</sub> are distinct iff D > 0, i.e., iff some measurement distinguishes them.

**Conclusion:** L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires operational distinguishability. P is a physical proposition only if P-true and P-false are operationally distinguishable.

### 4.4 Why This Is Not Operationalism By Stipulation

The objection: "You've just defined configurations operationally and then claimed L<sub>3</sub> requires operational distinguishability. That's circular."

The response: The distinguishability metric D is not an arbitrary choice. It is what Identity requires. If two configurations were indistinguishable in every respect, Identity would make them the same configuration. The metric D is the formal expression of "differ in some respect." Operational distinguishability is not stipulated; it is derived from what "distinct" means when L<sub>3</sub> is constitutive.

The deeper point: measurements are not external probes that we apply to a pre-existing reality. Measurements are the physical interactions through which configurations manifest their distinctness. If no interaction could in principle distinguish c<sub>1</sub> from c<sub>2</sub>, then there is no respect in which c<sub>1</sub> and c<sub>2</sub> differ, and Identity makes them one configuration.

This is why L<sub>3</sub>'s constitutive status is essential. If L<sub>3</sub> were merely regulative (a rule for reasoning), the objector could say: "The configurations are distinct in themselves, even if no measurement reveals the distinction." But if L<sub>3</sub> is constitutive (defining what counts as a configuration), the objector's position is incoherent: distinctness without any difference is not distinctness.

## 5. The Physical Proposition Criterion

### 5.1 Formal Statement

**Physical Proposition Criterion (PPC):** A claim Q counts as a physical proposition if and only if:

1. Q is about a configuration c ∈ A<sub>Ω</sub>.
2. Q satisfies L<sub>3</sub> (has determinate content, distinct truth-value states, determinate truth value).
3. Q-true and Q-false are operationally distinguishable (there exists a physically admissible measurement M such that P_M(Q-true) ≠ P_M(Q-false)).

**Epistemic Status:** ESTABLISHED within LRT, given:
- L<sub>3</sub>'s constitutive status (Section 2)
- The content requirements of L<sub>3</sub> (Section 3)
- The distinguishability structure of I<sub>∞</sub> (Section 4)

### 5.2 Scope

The PPC applies to all propositions about configurations in A<sub>Ω</sub>. It does not apply to:

- Formal propositions (mathematics, logic itself)
- Propositions about I<sub>∞</sub> as a whole (these are meta-level statements about the framework)
- Propositions about non-physical domains (if any exist)

### 5.3 The Failure Condition

A claim Q fails the PPC when Q has the grammatical form of a proposition but lacks an operational signature. Such claims are not physical propositions. They are grammatical expressions without physical content.

Examples:
- "The electron has a definite position even though no measurement could reveal it" (fails: no operational signature for "definite position" beyond measurement)
- "The composite system has a holistic property not accessible via local measurements" (fails: if not locally accessible, then no measurement distinguishes it, then not a physical property under L<sub>3</sub>)
- "There is a fact of the matter about which path the photon took, even though interference shows no which-path information" (fails: interference demonstrates no operational signature for which-path claims)

## 6. Addressing the Critic

### 6.1 The OT Position Revisited

Operational Transcendence holds that physical reality may outstrip all possible operational access. Section 1.2 acknowledged this as a coherent position.

The derivation in Sections 2-4 shows why LRT rejects OT: OT requires distinctness without difference. If two states of affairs are distinct (Non-Contradiction), they must differ in some respect (Identity), and if they differ, their difference is captured by the distinguishability structure of I<sub>∞</sub> (since that structure just is what difference means for configurations).

OT is not refuted by showing a logical contradiction. It is excluded by LRT's constitutive reading of L<sub>3</sub>. A critic who holds OT does not disagree with an inference within LRT; they reject LRT's foundational commitment.

### 6.2 The Hidden Variable Case

The hidden variable theorist claims: particles have definite positions even when no measurement reveals them.

LRT's response: "definite position" is either a physical proposition or it isn't. If it is, the PPC requires an operational signature: some measurement, in some context, must distinguish position-here from position-there. If no such measurement exists even in principle, then "definite position" is not a physical proposition. It is a metaphysical posit without content in A<sub>Ω</sub>.

This does not refute Bohmian mechanics, which does provide an operational signature: the positions are revealed by measurement outcomes. The Bohmian claim is that positions are always definite, not merely when measured. LRT's response: the "always definite" claim is either operationally distinguishable from "only definite when measured" or it isn't. If distinguishable, let's see the measurement. If not distinguishable, the claim is not a physical proposition.

### 6.3 The Structural Realist Case

The structural realist claims: relational facts between distant systems are determinate even when no local measurement accesses them.

LRT's response: this is precisely the H1→H2 question addressed in the companion supplement. The short answer: if the relational fact is part of the composite system's identity (H1), then it must be operationally accessible (PPC); if accessible, it's locally accessible (because it supervenes on local facts by H1); hence local tomography (H2).

The structural realist who accepts that relations are part of physical identity must accept their operational accessibility. The structural realist who denies operational accessibility denies that relations are part of physical identity. Choose.

## 7. Relation to Other Supplements

### 7.1 S2 (H1→H2 Bridge)

S1 provides the foundation for S2. The H1→H2 bridge invokes "Operational Determinacy" as the missing premise. S1 derives Operational Determinacy (which is just the PPC) from L<sub>3</sub>'s constitutive status. With S1 in place, S2's argument is complete.

### 7.2 S3 (Eigenvalue Restriction)

S1 supports S3 indirectly. The eigenvalue restriction argument claims that event operators must have Boolean spectra because A's selection is Boolean. This requires that "A selects outcome O" is a physical proposition. S1 ensures it is: A's selection has an operational signature (the measurement outcome itself).

### 7.3 S4-S6 (UNS, D2, G-Equivariance)

S1 provides the foundation for all downstream steps that invoke "determinate fact" language. Whenever the derivation claims that some condition must be determinate (unique next state, continuous identity, symmetry preservation), the PPC specifies what determinacy requires: operational distinguishability.

## 8. Summary

| Step | Content | Status |
|------|---------|--------|
| 1 | L<sub>3</sub> is constitutive, not regulative | Framework commitment |
| 2 | L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires distinct truth-value states | Derived from L<sub>3</sub>'s content |
| 3 | Distinctness in I<sub>∞</sub> means D > 0 | From distinguishability metric |
| 4 | D > 0 iff operationally distinguishable | Definition of D |
| 5 | Therefore L<sub>3</sub>-satisfaction requires operational distinguishability | QED |

The PPC is not a new axiom. It is what L<sub>3</sub>'s constitutive status entails when applied to the distinguishability structure of I<sub>∞</sub>. The derivation is not circular: it does not assume the PPC to prove the PPC. It derives the PPC from the more fundamental commitment that L<sub>3</sub> defines what counts as a configuration.
