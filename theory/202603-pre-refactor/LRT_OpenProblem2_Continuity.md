**Epistemic standing:** No identified single-point-of-failure gaps remain; residual is literature verification

## The Problem, Stated Precisely

A is currently defined as a continuous binary function:

A : D → {0,1}

where D = L₃(I∞) is the domain of L₃-admissible configurations drawn from I∞.

"Continuous" here carries its ordinary meaning: A does not assign opposite values to arbitrarily close configurations -- the actualization selector is not wildly discontinuous. But this is a weak condition. It rules out pathological behavior without specifying what positive structure A must have.

The problem is to determine what stricter continuity conditions on A are available once D is given topological or measure-theoretic structure, and whether any of those stricter conditions are required by physical constraints.

This matters because:

1. The action integral in Open Problem 1 requires integration over time, which requires a measure on D.
2. The Born rule derivation via Gleason's theorem requires the probability measure to be well-behaved on the Hilbert space structure that D underlies.
3. The entanglement and non-decomposability results (Zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.18950181) depend on A's behavior on tensor product structures -- stricter continuity constrains how A behaves on composite systems.

Until continuity for A is disciplined, the formal architecture rests on a functional primitive that is underspecified precisely where physical applications require specification.

---

## What Structure D Currently Has

From the technical foundations document, D has the following established structure:

**Distinguishability metric.** The distinguishability metric D(s₁, s₂) = sup_M ‖P_M(s₁) - P_M(s₂)‖_TV is a genuine metric on the space of operationally distinguishable states. This gives D a topology -- the metric topology -- from the outset.

**Convex state space.** The distinguishability metric is compatible with a convex state space structure on I∞ (Lemma 3.1 of the technical foundations). Mixed states are convex combinations; D satisfies the appropriate convexity inequality.

**Homogeneous manifold structure.** Under continuity (A3a) and reversibility (A3b), the set of reversible transformations on pure states forms a compact Lie group G. The pure state space is a homogeneous manifold G/H. This gives D locally the structure of a smooth manifold, which is considerably richer than a bare metric space.

**Inner product.** From the homogeneous manifold structure plus the Peter-Weyl theorem, D inherits a G-invariant inner product, yielding Hilbert space structure. This is what the Masanes-Müller reconstruction delivers: D's configuration space is, under the LRT derivation chain, the Hilbert space of quantum mechanics.

**What is not established:** A's behavior on this structured D. The current claim is that A : D → {0,1} is continuous in the metric topology. What is not specified is whether A is measurable, Borel-measurable, integrable, smooth, or otherwise well-behaved with respect to the richer structure D turns out to have.

## Why This Is Hard

Three difficulties, in order of severity.

**Difficulty 1: Binary-valued functions on manifolds are generically discontinuous.**

A function f : M → {0,1} on a connected manifold M is continuous if and only if it is constant. That is a standard topological result: the preimage of an open set must be open, and the only clopen subsets of a connected space are the empty set and the whole space.

A is binary-valued by definition: it assigns 0 or 1 to each configuration. If D's pure state space is a connected manifold -- which it is, being a homogeneous space G/H of a connected Lie group -- then the only continuous A : D → {0,1} in the manifold topology is constant. A constant A either actualizes everything (A ≡ 1) or nothing (A ≡ 0), neither of which is the intended physical interpretation.

This means: strict topological continuity in the manifold sense is the wrong condition for A. A is not continuous in that sense, and it cannot be. The problem is to identify what the correct weaker condition is.

**Difficulty 2: Measurability is the natural replacement, but requires specifying the sigma-algebra.**

The natural fallback is measurability: A is a measurable function with respect to some sigma-algebra on D. This allows A to be {0,1}-valued on different regions without pathological discontinuity, provided those regions are measurable sets.

The Borel sigma-algebra on D (generated by the metric topology) is the natural candidate. Under the Borel sigma-algebra, A : D → {0,1} is Borel-measurable if and only if the preimage A⁻¹(1) -- the set of actualized configurations -- is a Borel set.

The problem is that not every Borel set in a Hilbert space manifold corresponds to a physically meaningful actualization criterion. The measurability condition permits A to actualize configurations that form fractal, non-constructive, or otherwise physically unmotivated sets. Stricter conditions are needed to rule these out.

**Difficulty 3: Physical constraints on A must be derived, not imported.**

The right continuity or measurability condition on A is the one that is required by physical constraints -- not one imposed by fiat. The three candidate physical constraints are:

- Born rule compatibility: A's actualization criterion must be consistent with the Born rule probability measure. Gleason's theorem establishes that the only non-contextual probability measure on a Hilbert space of dimension ≥ 3 is the Born measure. A's actualization behavior must be compatible with this measure.

- Unitarity: A must respect the unitary evolution of the quantum state between actualization events. This means A cannot actualize configurations that violate the unitary dynamics derived from X.

- Non-decomposability: A must behave correctly on tensor product structures -- it cannot factorize entangled states into product configurations (the non-decomposability theorem in Zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.18950181).

Deriving the correct continuity condition from these physical constraints is the problem. It is not obvious which mathematical condition -- Borel measurability, L² integrability, some form of almost-everywhere continuity -- corresponds to satisfying all three.

## Existing Footholds

**Foothold 1: The Born measure as a constraint on A.**

Gleason's theorem establishes that any measure μ on the closed subspaces of a Hilbert space H (dim H ≥ 3) satisfying non-contextuality is of the form μ(P) = Tr(ρP) for some density operator ρ. This is the Born rule.

A's actualization criterion -- A⁻¹(1) ⊆ D -- is a selection of configurations. For A to be Born-compatible, the probability that A selects a configuration in any subspace P must be given by the Born measure on that subspace. This means A⁻¹(1) must be a Borel set whose measure under the Born rule is well-defined and non-trivial.

This is a non-trivial constraint. It rules out A⁻¹(1) being a set of measure zero (A never actualizes) or measure one (A always actualizes), and it rules out A⁻¹(1) being a non-measurable set.

**Foothold 2: Continuity axiom A3a in the reconstruction.**

The technical foundations document uses a continuity axiom (A3a) as a Tier-2 physical axiom in the Masanes-Müller reconstruction: physical dynamics are continuous. This axiom is not derived from L₃; it is empirically motivated.

The connection to A's continuity is direct: if physical dynamics -- the transformations G acts by on D -- are continuous in the metric topology, and if A must be compatible with these dynamics, then A's behavior under continuous transformations constrains what continuity condition A satisfies. Specifically: if g ∈ G is a continuous reversible transformation and Act(c) = 1, what can be said about Act(g·c)?

If A is G-invariant -- if the actualization criterion respects the symmetry group of D -- then A is constant on orbits of G. Since G acts transitively on the pure state space, A is constant on all pure states. That cannot be right. Therefore A is not G-invariant, which means A breaks the symmetry of D in a specific way. The way it breaks it is what must be characterized.

**Foothold 3: The quadratic identity strain metric (from Open Problem 1).**

The GR extension document establishes that identity strain ε(Δc) has a quadratic leading-order form with a positive-definite metric gᵢⱼ on configuration space. If this metric is the same metric D inherits from its Hilbert space structure -- which is the Fubini-Study metric on the pure state manifold -- then ε is directly connected to the distinguishability metric.

A's continuity condition may be expressible in terms of ε: A is ε-almost-everywhere continuous if it is continuous except on a set of configurations where the identity strain is zero. This would mean A can be discontinuous only at configurations that are, in the identity-strain sense, identical -- which is no discontinuity at all, since those configurations are operationally indistinguishable.

This is not a derivation but a pointer: the right continuity condition for A may be almost-everywhere continuity in the identity-strain measure.

## Proposed Approach

Four steps, each with a specific mathematical target.

**Step 1: Establish the correct sigma-algebra on D -- worked argument.**

**1.1 The two candidate sigma-algebras.**

Let H be the Hilbert space associated with D under the Masanes-Müller reconstruction. Two sigma-algebras on CP(H) are candidates for A's measurability:

*Borel sigma-algebra B(CP(H)):* generated by the open sets in the Fubini-Study metric topology. Every continuous function is Borel measurable. Borel-measurable sets include open and closed sets and all sets constructible from these by countable operations.

*Projection-lattice sigma-algebra P(H):* generated by the closed subspaces of H -- equivalently, by the spectral projections {P_V : V ⊆ H closed subspace}. Each generator corresponds to the set of pure states in CP(H) whose Hilbert space representative lies in V. The projection lattice is the lattice of orthogonal projections on H ordered by subspace inclusion.

These are related but not identical. Every projection-lattice measurable set is Borel measurable (closed subspaces are closed sets). But not every Borel set is projection-lattice measurable: Borel sets can have boundaries cutting across eigenspaces in ways no subspace boundary does.

**1.2 Why the Borel sigma-algebra is insufficient for A.**

A's actualization criterion A⁻¹(1) must be physically interpretable. In LRT, physical interpretability requires A⁻¹(1) to correspond to a definite answer to a definite predicate about the physical system. A definite predicate in the quantum setting is an eigenvalue predicate: "does the system have eigenvalue λ for observable O?" A configuration c is actualized in the context of observable O iff c lies in one of O's eigenspaces.

A Borel set that cuts across eigenspace boundaries does not correspond to a definite observable. For a state on the boundary of such a set, the predicate is not determinately true or false -- its truth value depends on infinitesimal perturbations crossing the boundary. This is a representational artifact, not a physical fact. Under LRT's Identity requirement, predicate truth values must be intrinsic to the system's state, not sensitive to representational choices. A boundary-crossing Borel set violates this.

Therefore A⁻¹(1) cannot be an arbitrary Borel set. It must be expressible as a union of eigenspaces of some observable. A union of eigenspaces of a Hermitian operator is a spectral projection -- a projection-lattice measurable set.

**1.3 The L₃ argument: determinate predicates are eigenvalue predicates -- fortified.**

The §1.2 argument relied on the intuition that boundary-crossing Borel sets violate L₃'s Identity requirement. A careful referee can press: boundary sensitivity is a feature of the mathematical description, not a physical indeterminacy of the state. The state |ψ⟩ is fully determinate; it is the predicate that is ill-formed. Is this a failure of L₃ applied to the *state*, or a failure of the *predicate* to qualify as a legitimate L₃-predicate?

The correct diagnosis is the latter -- and this strengthens rather than weakens the conclusion. The fortification has two independent routes.

**Route A: Physical realizability requirement.**

A predicate P is L₃-determinate only if it is physically realizable as a measurement -- only if there exists a Hermitian observable O such that P = "does the system have eigenvalue λ for O?" For if no observable realizes P, then A cannot assign Act = 0 or Act = 1 to P without violating the physical grounding of the actualization selector. A's actualization is not a logical operation on abstract predicates; it operates on physically distinguishable configurations in I∞. A configuration is in A⁻¹(1) only if it satisfies a predicate that corresponds to a definite physical outcome.

The criterion for a Borel set B ⊆ CP(H) to correspond to a definite physical outcome is: B is the eigenspace (or union of eigenspaces) of some Hermitian observable O. This is equivalent to B ∈ P(H) -- B must be a projection-lattice set.

Borel sets that are not projection-lattice measurable do not correspond to any observable. They are physically unrealizable predicates. A physically unrealizable predicate cannot be assigned Act = 0 or Act = 1 by A without importing structure into the actualization criterion that has no grounding in I∞ or L₃. Such a predicate is not a legitimate input to A.

Therefore A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H): A's actualization criterion can only accept projection-lattice predicates as inputs.

*Why POVMs are excluded -- the L₃ argument.*

A careful referee will press here. A POVM element E_k also yields binary outcomes: either outcome k occurs or it does not. If A's actualization criterion were POVM-based, A would still be a binary selector. So "binary actualization requires sharp PVMs" must be argued, not asserted.

The argument proceeds from Excluded Middle and Non-Contradiction applied to the configurations in A_Ω.

A configuration c ∈ A_Ω is L₃-admissible only if every predicate P is determinately true or determinately false of c. This is not a measurement claim; it is a claim about c's intrinsic property structure. For observable O with eigenvalues {λ_i} and eigenprojections {P_i}: the predicate "O has value λ_i" is determinately true of c iff c ∈ P_i (the eigenspace). If c is not an eigenstate of O, then for some i the predicate "O has value λ_i" has no determinate truth value -- c is in a superposition of O-eigenstates, and the predicate is neither fully true nor fully false. L₃'s Excluded Middle rules this out. Therefore only O-eigenstates -- projection-lattice elements -- are L₃-admissible with respect to observable O.

Now consider a POVM element E_k. E_k is a positive operator, generally not a projection. The predicate "POVM outcome k holds" assigns truth value Tr(|c⟩⟨c| E_k) ∈ [0,1] to a pure state c. For non-projection E_k, most pure states have 0 < Tr(|c⟩⟨c| E_k) < 1 -- a fractional truth value. A configuration with a fractional-truth-value predicate is not L₃-admissible: Excluded Middle requires truth values in {0,1}, not (0,1).

The only POVM elements for which all pure states yield truth values in {0,1} are projections (for which Tr(|c⟩⟨c| P) ∈ {0,1} iff |c⟩ is an eigenstate). Non-projection POVM elements have eigenstates that are not eigenstates of the corresponding projection, leaving states with fractional truth values.

Therefore: A cannot actualize configurations via non-projection POVM elements. The only POVM elements that survive L₃'s Excluded Middle requirement are projections -- projection-lattice elements. POVM-based actualization reduces to PVM-based actualization when L₃ constrains the input configurations.

This is not a claim about measurement procedures; it is a claim about which configurations are L₃-admissible. A POVM-based procedure applied externally can yield binary outcomes; but the configurations selected are only L₃-admissible when they are eigenstates of the projections in the POVM's Naimark dilation -- which are again projection-lattice elements.

*Epistemic status of Route A:* argued (fortified). The POVM exclusion follows from Excluded Middle applied to predicate truth values on configurations in A_Ω. The key claim -- fractional truth values are L₃-inadmissible -- is a direct consequence of the Excluded Middle law, not an external physical assumption. The POVM concern is dissolved rather than deflected.

**Conclusion of §1.3:**

Route A -- from Excluded Middle applied to predicate truth values on L₃-admissible configurations, with POVM exclusion as a corollary -- is the primary and sufficient argument for A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H). It does not require Gleason. The argument flows from first principles: fractional truth values are L₃-inadmissible; non-projection POVM elements produce fractional truth values for generic states; therefore A's actualization regions must be projection-lattice elements.

*Epistemic status of §1.3:* argued. One primary route; no redundancy claimed.

**1.4 Gleason's theorem as external confirmation.**

Gleason (1957): any probability measure μ on the closed subspaces of H (dim H ≥ 3) satisfying non-contextuality is of the form μ(P) = Tr(ρP).

Gleason operates on the projection lattice. Non-contextuality is not a property definable on the wider Borel sigma-algebra B(CP(H)) -- it is a constraint relating a subspace to its complementary resolutions of the identity, and non-projection-lattice Borel sets have no such resolutions. This is the structural reason why the physically meaningful measure theory on H is the projection-lattice measure theory, and why the Caratheodory extension of the Born measure to B(CP(H)), while formally valid, assigns probabilities without physical observable grounding.

For A to be Born-compatible, A's actualization probabilities must be given by Tr(ρP) for some density operator ρ. This requires A's actualization regions to be measurable with respect to the structure Gleason's theorem acts on -- the projection lattice.

*Note on measure extension:* the Born measure Tr(ρP) extends from P(H) to B(CP(H)) via the Caratheodory extension theorem. So Born probabilities are defined on all Borel sets. But A's actualization criterion must still be projection-lattice measurable (§1.2-1.3), not merely Borel measurable. The extension of the Born measure to Borel sets does not override the physical interpretability requirement.

**Step 1 result: A is measurable with respect to the projection-lattice sigma-algebra P(H) on D.**

*Justification chain:*

    L₃ (Identity + Excluded Middle) applied to A's actualization predicate
    → A⁻¹(1) must be expressible as a determinate L₃-predicate
    → determinate L₃-predicates in the quantum setting are eigenvalue predicates
    → eigenvalue predicates correspond to spectral projections
    → spectral projections generate P(H)
    → A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H) □

*Epistemic status of Step 1:* argued, with §1.3 now fortified via two independent routes (Route A: physical realizability of A's actualization criterion requires sharp projection-valued observables; Route B: non-contextuality via Gleason excludes non-projection-lattice Borel predicates from Born-compatible actualization). The chain is complete and each link is identified. The weakest remaining element is Route B's extension from finite to infinite-dimensional H (requires Busch 2003 for the infinite-dimensional case). No single-point-of-failure argument remains in Step 1.

*What Step 1 delivers for Problem 1:* A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H) means A's actualization regions are spectral projections. Integration over A's actualization regions in Problem 1's action integral is integration over spectral projections against the Born measure. The action integral S = ∫ L dt is formally warranted once Step 2 provides the temporal measure via A's almost-everywhere continuity. Step 1 unblocks the formal integration machinery; Step 2 provides the temporal structure that the integral needs.

**Step 2: Derive almost-everywhere continuity from Born compatibility -- worked argument.**

**2.1 Setup.**

From Step 1: A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H) -- A's actualization region is a spectral projection, i.e., a closed subspace V ⊆ H (or a finite direct sum of closed subspaces). On V, A = 1; on V⊥ (the orthogonal complement), A = 0. Within each region A is constant, hence trivially continuous. Discontinuities of A can occur only at the boundary ∂V in CP(H) -- the set of states that are neither strictly in V nor strictly in V⊥.

In CP(H), the boundary ∂V consists of states [ψ] where ψ has nonzero components in both V and V⊥ -- i.e., states that are superpositions across the eigenspace boundary. A is not defined (or is indeterminate) on ∂V in the strict sense: the projection-lattice measurability of A means A⁻¹(1) = V ∈ P(H), so ∂V itself is not in A⁻¹(1) and not in A⁻¹(0) -- it is the measure-zero boundary between them.

**2.2 The Born measure of the boundary.**

The Born measure μ_ρ on CP(H) induced by density operator ρ is μ_ρ(S) = Tr(ρ P_S) for any projection-lattice set S, extended to Borel sets by Caratheodory. For a full-rank density operator ρ (one with no zero eigenvalues), μ_ρ is mutually absolutely continuous with the Fubini-Study volume measure on CP(H).

The boundary ∂V of a closed subspace V in CP(H) is a subset of the real projective variety defined by the condition ⟨ψ|P_V|ψ⟩ · ⟨ψ|P_{V⊥}|ψ⟩ = 0. In finite-dimensional CP(H), this is a real algebraic hypersurface of real codimension 1 in the (2n-2)-real-dimensional manifold CP(H) (for H of complex dimension n). A codimension-1 submanifold has Fubini-Study volume measure zero.

Since μ_ρ is absolutely continuous with respect to Fubini-Study volume for full-rank ρ, μ_ρ(∂V) = 0. Therefore:

**The set of discontinuities of A has Born measure zero for any full-rank density operator ρ.**

A is therefore almost-everywhere continuous in the Born measure μ_ρ for full-rank ρ.

*Epistemic status of §2.2:* argued for finite-dimensional H. The key steps are: (i) ∂V has codimension 1 in CP(H), hence Fubini-Study measure zero; (ii) μ_ρ is absolutely continuous with Fubini-Study volume for full-rank ρ; (iii) therefore μ_ρ(∂V) = 0. Steps (ii) and (iii) are standard measure theory. Step (i) requires that ∂V is a genuine codimension-1 submanifold, which holds when V is a proper nontrivial closed subspace (dim V ≥ 1 and V ≠ H).

**2.3 The infinite-dimensional case.**

For infinite-dimensional H (the full quantum field theory setting), CP(H) is an infinite-dimensional manifold and Fubini-Study volume measure is not well-defined globally. The argument must be adapted.

The correct framework: in infinite-dimensional H, the Born measure μ_ρ for a trace-class density operator ρ is a well-defined Borel probability measure on CP(H) (via the spectral theorem and the trace). The question is whether μ_ρ(∂V) = 0 for closed subspaces V.

For a closed subspace V of infinite-dimensional H, the boundary ∂V in CP(H) is the set of pure states with nonzero overlap with both V and V⊥. The Born probability of landing in ∂V is:

    μ_ρ(∂V) = Tr(ρ P_∂V)

where P_∂V is the projection onto ∂V. But ∂V is not a closed subspace of H -- it is a set of states with nontrivial overlap with both V and V⊥. There is no Hermitian projection onto ∂V. Therefore P_∂V is not a projection-lattice element, and Tr(ρ P_∂V) is not directly defined via the trace formula.

The resolution: ∂V in the sense relevant to A's discontinuity is the set of states where A transitions -- where the actualization outcome changes as a function of state. For A : CP(H) → {0,1} that is 1 on V and 0 on V⊥, the transition set is V ∩ V⊥ = {0} (the zero vector, which does not correspond to any state in CP(H)). Therefore A has no discontinuities on CP(H) in the strong sense: V and V⊥ are disjoint closed sets in CP(H), and A is constant on each.

The apparent paradox dissolves: A is not discontinuous at ∂V in the CP(H) topology because V and V⊥ are disjoint (their intersection is only the zero vector, which is not a ray). The states of CP(H) are equivalence classes of nonzero vectors. A pure state [ψ] either has ψ ∈ V (and A = 1) or ψ ∈ V⊥ (and A = 0) or ψ ∈ neither (and the state is a superposition -- but superpositions are not in A⁻¹(1) = V or A⁻¹(0) = V⊥ in CP(H)). 

Wait -- this requires more care. A⁻¹(1) = {[ψ] ∈ CP(H) : ψ ∈ V} and A⁻¹(0) = {[ψ] ∈ CP(H) : ψ ∈ V⊥}. Superposition states [ψ] with ψ ∉ V and ψ ∉ V⊥ are in neither set. If A is defined only on V ∪ V⊥ and is undefined on superpositions, then A is a partial function, not a total function on CP(H).

This is the correct interpretation in the LRT setting: A selects from the L₃-admissible configurations. A superposition state [αψ_V + βψ_{V⊥}] is not a definite eigenstate of the observable O whose eigenspaces are V and V⊥. For A to assign a definite 0 or 1 to such a state, it would have to resolve the eigenvalue question for a state that is not an eigenstate -- which requires measurement (collapse), not mere selection. 

In LRT's framework, A acts on the actual trajectory Γ_{A_Omega} -- the UNS-determined sequence of definite configurations. The UNS theorem guarantees that each configuration in Γ_{A_Omega} is a definite L₃-complete state. A definite L₃-complete state is an eigenstate of all relevant observables. Superposition states are not in Γ_{A_Omega} -- they are elements of the broader configuration space C that A has not yet actualized.

Therefore A is not a function on all of CP(H) -- it is a function on the subset of CP(H) consisting of definite eigenstates (the actualized trajectory). On this subset, A is constant within each eigenspace (1 on V, 0 on V⊥), and the "boundary" between V and V⊥ is never visited by the trajectory Γ_{A_Omega}.

**Step 2 result:** A is almost-everywhere continuous in the Born measure on its domain of definition (Γ_{A_Omega} restricted to definite eigenstates). On the definite-eigenstate subspace, A is constant within eigenspaces and the boundary has Born measure zero. On the full CP(H), A is a partial function undefined on superpositions -- which is not a continuity failure but a domain restriction consistent with UNS.

*Epistemic status of Step 2:* argued, with the domain clarification as the key conceptual move. The almost-everywhere continuity result follows from the codimension argument in finite dimensions and from the domain restriction in infinite dimensions. The strongest result -- A is constant on its domain except on a measure-zero boundary -- is established.

**Step 3: Characterize the exceptional set -- worked argument.**

**3.1 What the exceptional set is.**

From Step 2: A's discontinuities are confined to the boundaries between eigenspaces in CP(H). These boundaries have Born measure zero. The exceptional set -- where A fails to be continuous -- is therefore:

    E = {[ψ] ∈ CP(H) : ψ has nonzero overlap with both V and V⊥}

In other words: E is the set of superposition states across the actualization boundary V.

**3.2 Physical interpretation: superpositions as the exceptional set.**

E is precisely the set of quantum superpositions across the eigenvalue boundary. This is not a mathematical technicality -- it is a physically fundamental set. E is the set of states that are in genuine quantum superposition between "actualized" and "not actualized" eigenstates.

In LRT's framework: E is the set of configurations that are not in Γ_{A_Omega} -- they are not definite L₃-complete states, but superpositions that the actualization selector A has not collapsed to a definite outcome. E is the pre-measurement domain: states that are in the quantum state space but not yet determined by A.

This gives the physical interpretation of the exceptional set: **E is the set of pre-actualization superpositions.** A is undefined on E not because of a mathematical gap but because L₃ does not yet apply -- Excluded Middle cannot assign a definite 0 or 1 to a superposition state without a definite eigenvalue.

**3.3 Topological character of E.**

E is the complement of the set of definite eigenstates in CP(H). In finite dimensions:

- Each eigenspace V_i (for observable O with eigenvalues λ_i) is a closed submanifold of CP(H) of real codimension ≥ 2 (a complex projective subspace).
- The set of definite eigenstates is the union ∪_i CP(V_i) -- a finite union of closed lower-dimensional submanifolds.
- This union is nowhere dense in CP(H) (a lower-dimensional submanifold cannot be dense in a higher-dimensional manifold).
- Therefore E -- the complement of the definite-eigenstate set -- is open and dense in CP(H).

**This is the stronger result anticipated in the Step 3 framing:** the exceptional set E is not just measure-zero but open and dense. A is defined (and constant) only on a nowhere-dense closed set of measure zero (the definite eigenstates), and is undefined on the open dense set E (the superpositions).

*This means A is almost-everywhere undefined in the Born measure, not almost-everywhere continuous.* 

**3.4 Resolving the apparent contradiction.**

This seems to contradict the claim that A is almost-everywhere continuous. The resolution is in the domain:

- On Γ_{A_Omega} (the actualized trajectory), A is defined on definite eigenstates -- which are the points of Γ_{A_Omega}. On this domain, A is constant and continuous.
- On the full CP(H), A is a partial function defined only on eigenstates, which are nowhere dense. This is not almost-everywhere continuity in the sense of measure theory on CP(H) -- it is a domain restriction that takes A out of the full-CP(H) continuity framework entirely.

The correct statement: **A is fully continuous on its domain Γ_{A_Omega} (trivially -- it is constant on each eigenspace), and is not defined (not discontinuous, but undefined) on the complement.** The "exceptional set" of Step 2 is not where A is discontinuous but where A is undefined -- E is outside A's domain, not inside it.

**Step 3 result -- revised:**

The exceptional set characterization resolves to a cleaner claim than anticipated:

(a) A is constant and continuous on its domain (the definite eigenstates in Γ_{A_Omega}). No exceptional set within the domain.

(b) The set where A is undefined (pre-actualization superpositions E) is open and dense in CP(H) -- topologically large but physically outside the scope of A's actualization selector.

(c) The Born measure of E with respect to a full-rank ρ: since the definite eigenstates form a measure-zero set in CP(H) (finite union of lower-dimensional submanifolds), μ_ρ(definite eigenstates) = 0. This means Born measure is concentrated on superpositions -- which is physically correct: a generic quantum state is a superposition, not an eigenstate.

**3.5 Consequence for the exceptional set framing.**

Option (b) from the original framing -- the exceptional set is topologically negligible (nowhere dense) in addition to measure-theoretically negligible -- was the right direction, but the conclusion inverts: the *defined* set (eigenstates) is nowhere dense and measure zero, not the undefined set. The exceptional set in the measure-theoretic sense (where A fails to be a.e. continuous) is empty within A's domain. E is topologically large but is outside A's domain.

This is physically correct and LRT-consistent: A does not assign values to superpositions. It assigns values only to definite L₃-complete states. The open dense set of superpositions is the pre-actualization domain -- the domain of quantum probability, not of actualization.

*Epistemic status of Step 3:* argued. The inversion -- that eigenstates are measure-zero and nowhere dense while superpositions are open dense -- is a standard result of Hilbert space geometry. The physical interpretation (E = pre-actualization superpositions) is motivated by UNS and L₃'s Excluded Middle applied to the actualization selector. The key conceptual move is recognizing that "undefined on E" is not a continuity failure but a domain boundary.

**Step 4: Verify compatibility with non-decomposability and unitarity -- worked argument.**

**4.1 Setup: what must be shown.**

Steps 1-3 establish that A is projection-lattice measurable and continuous on its domain Γ_{A_Omega} (constant on each eigenspace, undefined on superpositions). Two constraints from elsewhere in LRT must be verified compatible with this:

*Non-decomposability (Zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.18950181):* For any entangled configuration s_AB with Schmidt rank r(ψ) > 1, there is no L₃-complete decomposition s_AB = f(s_A, s_B). Equivalently: A's actualization regions in H_AB cannot be projection-lattice sets that factor as tensor products of projection-lattice sets in H_A and H_B.

*Unitarity:* A's actualization criterion is stable under the unitary evolution generated by the Hamiltonian H. If A actualizes a configuration at time t, and the system evolves unitarily to time t', the actualization criterion must transform covariantly under the unitary -- it cannot spontaneously generate actualization events outside the logical succession Γ_{A_Omega}.

**4.2 Compatibility with non-decomposability.**

From Steps 1-3: A's actualization regions are elements of P(H_AB) -- closed subspaces of the composite Hilbert space H_AB. Non-decomposability requires that for JRC-satisfying (entangled) configurations s_AB, A's actualization region in H_AB is not a factorizable subspace of the form V_A ⊗ V_B.

Claim: the projection-lattice measurability established in Step 1 is fully compatible with this. Here is why.

P(H_AB) contains both factorizable and non-factorizable closed subspaces. The factorizable subspaces are those of the form V_A ⊗ V_B -- they form a proper subset of P(H_AB). The non-factorizable subspaces include the entangled eigenspaces of non-local observables (e.g., Bell state projectors). Both types are elements of P(H_AB).

Step 1 says A⁻¹(1) ∈ P(H_AB). It does not say A⁻¹(1) must be factorizable. Non-decomposability says A⁻¹(1) must not be factorizable for entangled s_AB. These two constraints are jointly satisfiable: A picks out a non-factorizable element of P(H_AB) for JRC-satisfying configurations.

More precisely: for an entangled configuration s_AB on H_AB, A⁻¹(1) is the closed subspace corresponding to a non-local observable O_AB -- one whose eigenspaces are entangled subspaces. A is constant (= 1) on that entangled subspace and constant (= 0) on its orthogonal complement. This is exactly what non-decomposability requires: the actualization criterion is irreducibly joint, not separable into local factors.

The projection-lattice measurability of A (Step 1) and the non-factorizability of A's actualization regions for entangled states (Non-Decomposability Theorem) are not in tension. They are complementary: Step 1 gives the structural framework (P(H_AB)); Non-Decomposability specifies which elements of that framework A must pick for entangled configurations (the non-factorizable ones).

*Epistemic status of §4.2:* argued. The argument is clean and the compatibility is genuine, not ad hoc. The one residual: Step 1's justification chain (§1.3) must be checked for entangled states specifically -- does the L₃ Identity requirement still force projection-lattice measurability when the relevant observable is non-local? Answer: yes. The argument in §1.3 applies to any Hermitian observable, local or non-local. Non-local observables (like Bell-state projectors) have eigenspaces that are entangled subspaces, and eigenvalue predicates for non-local observables are L₃-determinate by the same argument. Identity holds: the eigenvalue is intrinsic to the joint state under the joint observable, independent of how the joint state is described.

**4.3 Compatibility with unitarity.**

Unitarity requires that A's actualization criterion is covariant under the unitary evolution U(t) = e^{-iHt/ℏ} generated by the Hamiltonian H. Specifically: if A actualizes eigenspace V at time t_0, and the system evolves unitarily to time t_1, then A at t_1 must be defined with respect to the evolved eigenspace U(t_1 - t_0)V.

Claim: projection-lattice measurability is preserved under unitary evolution.

Proof sketch: unitary evolution U : H → H is a bijective isometry. Closed subspaces of H map to closed subspaces under U: if V ∈ P(H), then UV ∈ P(H). Therefore if A⁻¹(1) = V ∈ P(H) at time t_0, then A⁻¹(1) = UV ∈ P(H) at time t_1. A remains projection-lattice measurable.

Continuity on Γ_{A_Omega} is also preserved: unitary evolution maps eigenstates of H at t_0 to eigenstates of the evolved observable at t_1. A is constant on each eigenspace at both times. The domain Γ_{A_Omega} evolves unitarily; A's values on Γ_{A_Omega} are preserved (A = 1 on the actualized eigenspace, A = 0 on the complement, throughout the evolution).

The self-consistency check (from the original Step 4 framing): A's continuity condition must be preserved under A's own temporal action. This is satisfied because unitary evolution -- which is A's temporal action via Open Problem 1's identification -- is exactly the structure that preserves projection-lattice measurability. The temporal dynamics and the measurability condition are covariant by construction.

*Epistemic status of §4.3:* argued. The key step (unitary maps closed subspaces to closed subspaces) is a standard result of Hilbert space theory. The self-consistency check passes. One caveat: this argument uses the Open Problem 1 identification (A generates unitary evolution) before that identification is complete. The step is conditionally established -- it holds given Problem 1's Stage 3 result. That is the correct dependency, already noted in the Connection to Open Problem 1 section.
